RIVER MARGINS

Literary critic Minh An discusses a new anthology of short stories from the Mekong Delta.

Until I began working on this anthology, I had assumed that I knew what a “Delta story” would look like. I expected boats, floating markets, rain-heavy afternoons and old women remembering a disappearing world. What I found was less decorative and far more unsettling. Some of the strongest stories contained no river scene at all. They took place in rented rooms, garment workshops, classrooms and bus stations. What made them belong to the Delta was not scenery, but a particular arrangement of pressure: land that had been sold, accents softened for school, daughters sent to the city, and families learning to treat departure as a form of loyalty. In that sense, the stories corrected my own lazy expectations before I had even finished reading them.

That mistake is not hard to explain. Writers from capital cities are often allowed to write as if they came from nowhere in particular; their streets are treated as modern life itself. Regional writers are granted no such freedom. Their work is praised for “local colour” before it is examined for structure, irony or moral intelligence. A story set in the Delta is therefore expected to prove its origin almost immediately. The river must appear, the dialect must be audible, and the poverty must be recognisable. This may seem harmless, but it quietly narrows what readers are prepared to notice.

One manuscript in the collection made this especially clear. It was built around the notebook of a seventeen-year-old factory worker who had moved from a riverside town to the edge of Ho Chi Minh City. The astonishing thing was not that she missed home, nor that she wanted independence. It was how contemporary her embarrassment sounded. She worried about speaking “too provincially” in front of classmates, about sending money home without seeming trapped by obligation, and about whether success required her to become less recognisably herself. These anxieties could have belonged to a student today. The supposed distance between “rural tradition” and “modern youth” suddenly looked much thinner than many readers might assume.

To test this, I once gave two groups of students different versions of the same story. One version kept every dialect spelling, every river reference and every mention of the floating market. The other removed most of these surface markers while leaving the plot, conflicts and emotional structure unchanged. Predictably, the first group called their version “more authentic”. More surprisingly, they also judged it less serious. The local signs helped them identify the setting, but they also encouraged them to treat the story as cultural illustration rather than literature. The experiment did not prove that dialect or landscape should be removed. It showed that familiar signals can train readers to stop thinking too soon.

This is why I have avoided choosing stories merely because they look recognisably “southern”. A canal, a fruit boat or a comic rustic character may locate a story quickly, but speed is not the same as understanding. The best writers in this volume allow regional identity to emerge through hesitation, silence, obligation and rhythm. Their sense of place is not announced but absorbed. I have also tried not to let the familiar postcard version of the Delta decide what belongs here. A story about a child hiding her accent in a classroom may be as deeply regional as one about a family living beside a river.

Translation makes the problem harder. Several stories carry traces of Khmer, Chinese-Vietnamese or local speech beneath the Vietnamese surface; a few have been translated from community publications with very small readerships. Translation makes these works available to more readers, but it also changes their temperature. A joke may remain funny while losing its social sharpness. A term of address may become clear but less intimate. I do not regard this as a reason to avoid translation. Rather, it is a reason to read translated regional writing with humility, aware that what has become accessible has also been altered.

What worries me most is not that readers know too little about the Delta, but that they often know too quickly. The region has repeatedly been made scenic before it has been heard. If this anthology has any value, it will not be because it explains the Delta once and for all. It will be because it makes readers slower to turn it into scenery. To read these stories well is to listen before arranging it, to accept complication before recognition, and to stay with its difficulty long enough for the people in the stories to become more than representatives of a place.

WHY VEGAN CHEESE STILL HASN’T HAD ITS OAT-MILK MOMENT

A. A decade ago, plant-based food looked less like a niche diet than a cultural and financial movement. Wellness influencers, environmental documentaries and celebrity investors helped make vegan alternatives seem both morally attractive and commercially inevitable. Plant-based milk benefited most from this shift. Oat milk, in particular, became common in cafés and supermarkets, and many consumers who were not vegan began ordering it as a normal part of their routine. Vegan cheese, however, has remained the awkward exception. Although dairy-free ice cream, chocolate and milk alternatives have become widely available, vegan cheese still occupies only a tiny share of the cheese market. Its problem is not merely that shoppers are conservative. It is that cheese is unusually difficult to imitate.

B. The difficulty begins with casein, the milk protein that gives cheese many of its defining qualities. Unlike many proteins, casein does not hold a rigid fixed shape. It gathers into tiny clusters that trap fat and calcium, helping cheese melt, stretch and change flavour as it ages. Plant proteins behave differently. They tend to be more rigid and do not bind fat and calcium in the same way. For this reason, many companies have tried to imitate what cheese does rather than what cheese is. They often use coconut oil to create fat and melt, and tapioca starch to provide stretch. These substitutes can work reasonably well on a burger or pizza, but they struggle when the cheese has to stand on its own. The result can be waxy, bland, nutritionally weak and sometimes faintly coconut-like.

C. One possible answer is to make casein without using a cow. This is the promise of precision fermentation, a process in which microorganisms such as bacteria or yeast are genetically instructed to produce a particular compound. The method is not new: in the 1980s, related techniques were used to produce synthetic insulin. At Wageningen University in the Netherlands, researchers have inserted the DNA sequence responsible for cow casein into E. coli bacteria. Fed with glucose, the bacteria produce caseins that can then be mixed with oil and water to form a mozzarella-like material. Yet the science is ahead of the market. Under European Union rules, precision-fermented cheese is treated as a novel food and must be approved before being sold to consumers. Some countries also take a cautious view of tasting such products before approval, although the Netherlands has begun to allow limited tastings under strict conditions.

D. While European laboratories worked slowly through regulatory caution, Silicon Valley tried a more flamboyant route. Oliver Zahn, a former data scientist who had worked at Google, SpaceX and Impossible Foods, founded Climax Foods in Berkeley in 2019. He argued that vegan cheese could be improved by treating recipes as a data problem. The company presented itself as a “future of food” enterprise, promising to use machine learning to predict how different ingredients would behave. Zahn’s team began with speciality cheeses, partly because a premium product could impress investors before a cheaper mass-market version was ready. Their early showpiece was Climax Blue, a plant-based blue cheese made from seeds, beans and coconut cream. It looked convincing and attracted attention from chefs and investors, including Paul McCartney.

E. But blue cheese was not the hardest target. Its flavour could be helped by using mould, while popular cheeses such as mozzarella depended much more on physical performance: melt, stretch and protein structure. Then, in 2022, an accidental oven-temperature mistake produced what looked like a breakthrough. A plant-protein sample overheated and unexpectedly melted and stretched like dairy cheese. Climax called the new ingredient Caseed. It promised a vegan cheese that was not only stretchier but also higher in protein than existing oil-and-starch products. At roughly the same time, however, enthusiasm for plant-based food began to cool. Investors realised that food could not be scaled like software. Factories, margins and ingredient costs mattered. A promised large investment from a London fund failed to arrive, and Climax had to survive on smaller rounds of funding.

F. The company’s later history showed the gap between invention and business. Climax Blue appeared in respected restaurants and the French food group Bel announced plans to work with the company, but the expected roll-out never happened. Staff numbers fell, priorities shifted, and former employees later suggested that the company had tried to pursue too many products at once. One said Zahn seemed to be waiting for a “white knight” investor that would allow the company to keep doing everything. In 2024, new investors required Zahn to step down as chief executive. The firm was renamed Bettani Farms, dropped much of its tech-heavy image and began emphasising more familiar, “naturally derived” ingredients. Its new leadership focused on popular cheeses such as mozzarella and feta rather than Climax Blue. The story suggests that a brilliant food invention is not automatically a viable food business. Vegan cheese is still waiting for the breakthrough that made oat milk ordinary.
